What is Stationarity?¶

A stationary time series is one whose properties do not depend on the time at which the series is observed.¶

Hyndman, R.J., & Athanasopoulos, G. (2018) Forecasting: principles and practice, 2nd edition, OTexts: Melbourne, Australia. OTexts.com/fpp2/stationarity. Accessed on Dec 4, 2020.

Non-stationarity is often observed through changing mean or variance in the time series.

Why is Stationarity Important? A Motivating Example¶

Imagine the dataset created by a person trying to lose weight over a 10 week period. For this examle, assume the person needs to eat less than 2500k per day to lose weight. The person starts out strong, but then starts to lose steam as they get bored and hungry. They start eating a little more but stick to the diet generally.

In [215]:
avg_kcal_eaten = np.array([1500, 1600, 1550, 1750, 1810, 1860, 1650, 1500, 1970, 2000])
maintenance = 2500
weight_loss = ((2500 - avg_kcal_eaten)*7)/7700
weeks = np.arange(0,10)


weight = np.empty(len(weeks))
weight[0] = 70
for w in weeks:
    if w != 0:
        this_week = weight[w-1] - weight_loss[w]
        weight[w] = this_week
        
# Create a dataframe
df = pd.DataFrame({"weight":weight,"avg_kcal_eaten":avg_kcal_eaten}, index=weeks, dtype="float64")

def standardise(x):
    x = np.array(x)
    min_x = np.min(x)
    max_x = np.max(x)
    for i in range(0,x.shape[0]):
        x[i] = (x[i] - min_x) / (max_x - min_x)
    return x


plt.plot(standardise(df['weight']))
plt.plot(standardise(df['avg_kcal_eaten']))
plt.xlabel("# Weeks")
plt.ylabel("Standardised Value")
plt.title("Weight vs Kcal Eaten Over Time")

Why is this happening?¶

When the target variable is non-stationary the results are often unexpected or incorrect. Lets look at the calculation of the correlation to see why this is the case.

The formula above shows the formula for the Pearson correlation. Notice that the formula takes each observation and subtracts the mean. But if there is a trend, the "mean" is constantly changing after every observation.

In this case, the mean cuts a line right through the middle and we end up simply associating the 2 sections above and below the line.

In order to correct for this issue, we need to do something called 'detrending'. Effectively, we want to first remove the increasing trend from the series. In the next section, Juan will show a number of methods to do this on real problems.

For now, let us approach the problem logically and just say that is not the absolute value of the weight that is important to us, but rather the rate of weight loss per week.
